Start here

Why tunnels exist

On a blockchain, every payment is a transaction: the whole network has to agree on it. That's fine for one payment. It's terrible if two people (or two AI agents) want to pay each other hundreds of times a second — you'd drown in fees and waiting.

The analogy — a bar tab

You don't pay the bartender for each drink. You open a tab, they keep a running total, and you settle once when you leave. A tunnel is a bar tab between two parties, enforced by math instead of trust.

Same 100 payments. Without a tunnel: 100 transactions, 100 fees, 100 waits. With a tunnel: 2 transactions total, and everything in between is just two people signing messages to each other. Signing is instant and free — that's where "millions per second" comes from.

Step 1

Opening the tunnel

Before you can run a tab, you set it up on-chain. This is the first of the only two transactions.

1

Create the tunnel

Alice creates a shared Tunnel object and names both parties. For each party it records their address and their public key — the key it will later use to check that a message was really signed by them.

2

Both parties deposit

Alice puts in 0.06, Bob puts in 0.04. The money now lives inside the tunnel — a shared vault. Neither can take it out unilaterally.

3

The tunnel goes Active

Once both have funded, the tunnel flips to Active and its starting balances are just the deposits: Alice 0.06, Bob 0.04. From here on, everything happens off-chain.

Why record the public keys?

Later, every change to who-owns-what has to be signed by both parties. The tunnel stores their keys now so it can verify those signatures at settlement — without needing anyone online.

Step 2 · the core idea

A single off-chain update

This is the heart of the whole thing. A "payment" inside the tunnel is just a new agreement on the balances, that both people sign. It never touches the chain.

Every agreement is called a state. A state has four parts:

a

nonce — a version number

Starts at 1 and goes up by one every update. It tells everyone which agreement is newer. This one number is what makes cheating impossible (more in §6).

b

party_a_balance & party_b_balance

Who owns what, right now. If Alice pays Bob 0.01, the new state just says Alice has 0.01 less and Bob has 0.01 more.

c

state_hash — a private fingerprint

A short fingerprint of what happened off-chain (the messages, the game moves). The details stay private; only the fingerprint is committed, so the settlement can prove "this is the real history" without revealing it.

Key rule #1

A state only counts if both parties signed it. You cannot invent a balance in your own favour, because your counterparty's signature has to be on it too. No signature from them = not a valid state.

This is the "6M TPS"

Those updates are just signed messages between two parties. Sign a million of them a second and you have a million "transactions" a second — while Sui only ever settles the final one. Both numbers are real; they measure different things.

Step 4 · the happy ending

Settling (cooperative close)

When they're done, they cash out. This is the second and last transaction.

1

Take the latest signed state

That's nonce 5: Alice 0.070, Bob 0.030, with both signatures on it.

2

Submit it once

Either party sends close_cooperative(...) with those balances and both signatures. The contract checks the signatures and that the balances still sum to the deposit.

3

The vault pays out

The tunnel sends Alice 0.070 and Bob 0.030, and marks itself Closed. Done — dozens of payments, two on-chain transactions.

Step 5 · the unhappy ending

What if someone cheats or disappears?

The cooperative close needs both parties. What if Bob goes offline — or worse, tries to cash out an old state where he had more money? This is where the design earns its keep.

1

Bob cheats: he posts an old state

Bob calls raise_dispute with nonce 2 (Alice 0.064, Bob 0.036) — a moment when he had more. This starts a countdown timer.

2

Alice overrides with a newer state

Alice is watching. She calls resolve_dispute with nonce 4 (which they both signed) — a higher nonce. The contract accepts it because it's newer, and replaces Bob's stale claim.

3

The timer runs out

Nobody has an even-newer signed state, so nothing more can change.

4

Anyone force-closes

force_close pays out at the highest nonce seen — nonce 4, the honest state. Bob's cheat gained him nothing.

The one catch — you must be watching

The safety depends on the honest party responding within the dispute window. In real systems, "watchtower" services do this for you so you don't have to stay online. (In our live demo the window is ~20s, which is why that part pauses.)

Step 6

The four rules that make it safe

Everything above rests on four checks the contract enforces. If you understand these, you understand tunnels.

1

Both must sign

A state is only valid with both signatures. You can't move money to yourself without your counterparty agreeing.

2

The nonce only goes up

A newer (higher-nonce) signed state always beats an older one. So posting a stale, favourable state is pointless — it gets overridden.

3

Balances must conserve

Alice + Bob must always equal the total deposited. You can't conjure money out of the channel or leave some stranded.

4

Disputes have a timeout

The window gives the honest party time to override a stale claim, then finalizes. This is what makes unilateral exit safe when the other side vanishes.

Step 7 · going bigger

Paying someone you don't share a tunnel with

Alice has a tunnel with a router, the router has one with Bob. Can Alice pay Bob through the router without trusting it? Yes — with a hash lock (an "HTLC").

1

Bob picks a secret

Bob invents a secret s and shares only its fingerprint H = hash(s).

2

Locks are set along the path

Alice locks a payment to the router: "payable to whoever reveals a secret matching H." The router locks the same to Bob.

3

The secret unlocks it backward

Bob reveals s to claim from the router. The router now knows s, so it claims from Alice. Everyone gets paid — or the locks expire and refund. No hop can steal.

Step 8

The real contract, and how we found it

There's no SDK and no docs for the real thing. But the experiment settled on-chain, so its contract is public. From a single settlement transaction we recovered the whole 8-module ABI, then rebuilt the core ourselves and ran it on testnet.

We rebuilt tunnel; the rest add routing, trustless dispute (referee + ZK), primitives, and the demo's game domain.

09 · A real use case

Building a single-player racing game

Let's make it concrete: an on-chain racing game — one player, time trials, earn as you race. Here's exactly how a tunnel carries it, one piece at a time.

Reframe first — "1 player" still needs a second party

You race solo (against the clock or AI), but a tunnel always has two sides. The other side is the house — the game itself. You feel alone; the channel is you ↔ house. There is no tunnel with a single address.

Do you even need one?

Only if money moves many times — entry fees, payouts, credits. If your game has no economy, skip tunnels entirely: just play and post a score. A tunnel is for many paid rounds without a transaction each round.

Part A — Set up the game (on-chain, once)

1

You deposit a bankroll

Your playing credits — say 1.00. This is what you race with.

2

The house deposits a prize float

The game puts in 1.00 too, so it can actually pay your winnings. The tunnel now holds the whole pot.

3

Tunnel goes Active

Starting balances: you 1.00, house 1.00. From here, you play.

Part B — Each race (off-chain, fast, repeatable)

1

You race in the client

Normal real-time gameplay — steering, laps, a target time. None of this is on-chain.

2

The race ends → a new balance

Beat the target time? You win. Missed it, or paying to play? You lose the entry fee. Either way it's a small shift.

3

You both co-sign the result

A new state, nonce+1: win → you go up, house goes down; fee/loss → you go down, house goes up. Sign, and race again. No chain touched.

Part C — Cash out (on-chain, once)

1

Settle the net balance

Take the last co-signed state (you 1.10, house 0.90) and close_cooperative. You walk away with 1.10. Two hundred races would still be just two transactions: open and cash out.

Part D — Keeping the house honest

Co-signing already stops theft both ways: the house can't touch your credits without your signature, and you can't claim a win it didn't sign. If either side vanishes, the other force_closes at the last co-signed state. What's left is proving results and fair RNG — build it up in three levels:

v1

Trust the house's server

The house computes the result, you both sign it. Ship this first — it reuses the channel almost as-is.

v2

Deterministic replay

Make the race deterministic: same inputs + same seed → one outcome. Co-sign the inputs, and on a dispute a Move function recomputes the winner. Now you don't trust the house's word.

v3

ZK proof + verifiable RNG

Commit-reveal the seed so the house can't rig the track, and prove the outcome with a Groth16 proof. Fully trustless — exactly why the real contract ships randomness, referee, and zk_verifier.

The one-line version

Single-player = you vs the house, many rounds. The tunnel batches every round into one open + one settle; co-signing keeps either side from stealing; determinism + verifiable RNG keep the house honest about your results.

10 · The full architecture

Where everything actually lives

The surprise of the whole design: the off-chain states are stored nowhere global. No mempool, no server of record. Each party keeps their own copy, on their own device — and the newest co-signed state is your money claim. It's a bearer instrument: guard it like a key.

What is stored where
ThingLives onNote
Co-signed stateseach party's devicetwo copies in the world; only the NEWEST matters
The transcript (messages, moves)the partiesthe chain only ever sees its hash
Deposits + tunnel objectSuiplus whatever state was last submitted at settle/dispute
A backup of the latest statewatchtower (optional)so you can be offline during a dispute window

Two connections, two jobs: a fat one to your counterparty (signing, fast), a thin one to Sui (watching + settling, rare).

The trade-offs this creates

Synchronous: both parties must be online to advance state — a tunnel is a live session, not a mailbox. Durability: persist every new state (disk / DB / watchtower); our playground keeps them in browser memory, which is fine for a demo and wrong for production. Liveness vs safety: a dropped connection never loses funds — you just stop, reconnect, or force-close at the last co-signed state.

11 · Set it up from scratch

Building your own, step by step

The contract is the smallest part — and it's already written. Here's the whole path from nothing to a working tunnel app, in order.

1

Get the contract on-chain (no writing needed)

Reuse the published package (tunnels_edu::channel, testnet 0x9bd35322…) or publish your own copy: cd move && sui client publish. One command. The contract is generic — payments, chat, streaming and a game all ran through it unchanged.

2

Build the client

Port app/src/lib/tunnel.ts (~200 lines): keypairs, the BCS StateUpdateData message, coSign, and the five chain calls — create, deposit, close_cooperative, raise/resolve_dispute, force_close. This is where most of your work lives.

3

Wire the transport

A live connection between the parties so proposals and signatures can travel: the game's own WebSocket (player ↔ house), WebRTC (p2p), or a dumb relay. The relay is untrusted plumbing — it can drop messages but can't forge a state, because everything is signed.

4

Persist every state

On every co-signed update, write the newest state to disk / localStorage / your DB — and optionally send a copy to a watchtower. Losing it means trusting your counterparty's copy.

5

Watch the chain

During a dispute window someone must be able to answer with a higher nonce — you (poll or subscribe to the tunnel's events) or a watchtower doing it for you.

6

Settle — and clean up

Cooperative close with the latest state is the happy path (one tx). Force-close after the timeout is the exit when the other side vanishes. Sweep leftovers back to your treasury (the playground's ♻ Recover).

Do you ever write new Move?

Only when the chain must judge what happened, not just how much moved. Payments/chat/streaming → reuse as-is. A different token → a ~5-line Balance<T> edit. A game verdict ("did I win?") → write the resolver: referee (v1) → deterministic replay (v2) → ZK proof (v3). Multi-hop/multi-party → crib from the real contract's hop and referee modules.

Everything referenced here is in the repo: github.com/Nuel-osas/tunneling — move/ (the contract + tests), app/ (the playground, including the client lib and the funder endpoint), scripts/tunnel-e2e.mjs (the full flow as one readable script). And the live playground to poke: tunnel-playground.vercel.app.

Reference

Glossary

Tunnel (state channel)
A shared on-chain vault between two parties that lets them transact off-chain and settle the net result once.
State
One agreed snapshot of the balances, plus a nonce and a fingerprint — signed by both parties.
Nonce
A version number that increases every update. Higher nonce = newer = wins.
Co-signed
Signed by both parties. Only co-signed states are valid.
Cooperative close
The happy-path settlement: both agree on the final state and cash out in one transaction.
Dispute
Unilateral settlement when the other party won't cooperate. A timer + higher-nonce override protect the honest side.
HTLC
Hash Time-Locked Contract — a lock that pays whoever reveals a secret before a deadline. Lets payments route across hops safely.
state_hash
A fingerprint of the off-chain history, so settlement can commit to the truth without revealing the details.
